What Is It?
When people talk about Ebola, they are often referring to the Ebola Virus Disease (EVD), also known as Ebola Hemorrhagic Fever (EHF). In humans, the disease is caused by four of five viruses within the genus Ebolavirus. The Ebola Virus Disease caused by any of the four strains are extremely deadly, with mortality rates ranging from 40% to 90%. The deadliest is the Zaire Ebolavirus (EBOV) at 90%.

The ebolavirus is a virus, meaning it is not alive and cannot reproduce on its own. It must take body cells as its host cells to reproduce.
Symptoms
Incubation Period
The incubation period between first infection and first symptoms can be anywhere from 2-21 days, but is normally around 4-10 days long.
Days 1-5
The first symptoms that appear are similar to influenza symptoms. These may include:
  • Fever
  • Weakness
  • Muscle/Joint Pain
  • Headache
  • Sore Throat
Days 5-7
From days 5-7, symptoms worsen. The following symptoms may develop:
  • Vomitting
  • Diarrhea
  • Chest Pain
  • Trouble Breathing
  • Confusion
  • Skin Rashes
Days 7-10
At this point, the Ebola virus has infected many immune and organ cells, causing massive organ damage and internal and external bleeding.

Vomiting blood, coughing blood, blood in stool, and bleeding from the eyes, nose, mouth, and anus can occur. Heavy internal bleeding may occur in the gastrointestinal track, and there may be decreased blood clotting, bleeding from needle punctures, and bruising.
At this point, most patients die of low blood pressure due to fluid loss through bleeding, diarrhea, and other symptoms.

In some cases, the immune system of a patient is able to rebound and produce more white blood cells and antibodise to fight off the ebola infection.
How Ebola Attacks The Body
The ebola virus directly attacks immune cells, such as macrophages, dendritic cells, and memory cells. This prevents the immune system from immediately being able to fight the virus off with antibodies. The virus then goes on to infect various body cells, causing organ failure (particularly in the liver) and blood loss (particularly in the gastrointestinal tract).
Treatment and Prevention
The Ebola Virus spreads through transfer of bodily fluids such as blood, saliva, semen, and vomit.

The Ebola Virus is very hard for the immune system to fight off because it directly attacks guard and memory cells, preventing the immune system from mounting a defense against it and fight it off.

As of today, there is no approved cure or vaccine for the Ebola virus. A patient can only recover from it if their immune system is able to fight it off. To maximize the chance of survival, several methods are used.

Because death is mostly caused by fluid loss, patients infected with the ebola virus are put on IVs delivering fluid into their bloodstream and preventing blood pressure from dropping to dangerous levels. Oxygen therapy is also used to make sure that the body can get enough oxygen if the lungs are damaged. Blood transfusions are used to resupply lost bodily fluids. Any infections are treated to prevent further damage.

In the end, no drugs can fight off or suppress the virus and it is up to the immune system of the patient to produce more T-cells and memory cells that are able to produce antibodies to disable and destroy ebola viruses in the bloodstream.

Even after recovery, the patient may still be effected for the rest of their lives. The weight lost while infected might never be recovered, and weakness may persist. Chronic inflammation or blindness are possible lasting effects. Even if not in the bloodstream, the ebola virus can still be spread through semen or breastmilk for years after recovery.

Ebola only spreads through the transfer of bodily fluids, so it can be prevented by good hygiene and safety practices, such as washing hands, keeping surfaces clean, and using needles safely. Especially in developing countries, medical facilities often cannot meet these standards, allowing for ebola to spread easily; in countries with more resources, patients should be quarantined to prevent the further spread of ebola.
